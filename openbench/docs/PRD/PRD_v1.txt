1. Product thesis

OpenBench OS is a lab execution operating layer that turns dense lab documents into a live, safety-aware,
multimodal run companion.

The key framing: this is not "ChatGPT for protocols." It is closer to a protocol compiler + execution runtime +
visual checker + run historian.

A user uploads an SOP, SDS, equipment manual, and optional lab-specific notes. OpenBench OS compiles those
documents into a structured execution graph: steps, prerequisites, hazards, PPE, engineering controls, checkpoints,
stop conditions, escalation rules, timers, materials, expected observations, and reportable deviations. During
execution, it gives hands-free guidance, answers context-aware questions, asks for confirmation at critical gates,
accepts photos for visible setup checks, and generates a final handover report.

The PRD-level product promise:

      OpenBench OS helps scientists execute existing procedures more safely, accessibly, and traceably by
      converting approved lab documentation into a live, evidence-linked execution assistant.

The phrase "approved lab documentation" matters. It keeps the product away from "inventing experiments" and
toward a safer, defensible wedge: make existing protocols easier to follow, verify, and document.

2. Why this is a strong wedge

Labs already treat SOPs, PPE, SDSs, training, and activity-specific controls as core safety infrastructure. OSHA's
laboratory standard requires a Chemical Hygiene Plan to include safety-relevant SOPs and criteria for control
measures such as engineering controls, PPE, and hygiene practices. OSHA's Hazard Communication material also
makes SDSs and chemical hazard communication central to workplace chemical safety. (OSHA)

The National Academies' Prudent Practices in the Laboratory similarly frames chemical hygiene plans around
responsibilities, emergency preparedness, PPE, chemical management, housekeeping, SOPs, training, safety
equipment, and emergency action planning. It also emphasizes that lab personnel should follow SOPs, read
SDS/MSDS information before chemical use, wear appropriate PPE, and receive safety training before new work or
equipment use. (National Academies)

The accessibility angle is also real. WHO says access to assistive technology is blocked by barriers including low
awareness, high costs, limited physical access, inadequate product range, workforce capacity gaps, inadequate policy,
insufficient funding, and fragmentation. A hands-free, voice-first, multimodal lab companion is not just convenience;
it can support users whose lab work is constrained by gloves, mobility, visual load, cognitive load, or the need to
keep both hands on the procedure. (World Health Organization)

The "why now" angle is strong because current models are better suited to long-running, multimodal, source-
grounded workflows. Anthropic describes Opus 4.7 as improved for complex long-running tasks, instruction
following, verification, high-resolution image handling, and file-system memory, with image support up to 2576px /
3.75MP. That maps unusually well to "read long lab docs, keep run state, inspect a bench photo, and produce a
structured report." (Anthropic)

4. Target users and personas

Primary user: bench scientist / technician

This person is physically performing the work. They need fast, hands-free access to procedure context, hazard
reminders, timers, calculations, and deviation logging.

Pain points:

   · SOP, SDS, and equipment manual are separate documents.
   · Gloves and contamination risk make keyboard use inconvenient.
   · Documentation is often delayed until after the run.
   · Step-specific hazards are easy to miss when buried in long docs.
   · New staff may not know what should be escalated.

Core job to be done: "Help me follow the approved procedure without losing context, missing safety gates, or
forgetting what happened."

Secondary user: lab manager / PI

This person cares about reproducibility, onboarding, training, and handover quality.

Pain points:

   · New members ask repeated procedural questions.
   · Deviations are under-documented.
   · Handoffs between shifts are inconsistent.
   · People use outdated SOP versions or skip context.

Core job to be done: "Give my team a reliable execution layer that makes procedures easier to follow and deviations
easier to review."

Secondary user: EHS / safety officer

This person cares about safety policy, training, PPE, hazard controls, and incident traceability.

Pain points:

   · SOPs vary in quality.
   · PPE/control requirements are not always surfaced at point of use.
   · Photos and inspection evidence are hard to organize.
   · People may not know when to stop and escalate.

Core job to be done: "Make safety-critical requirements visible during execution and produce a record of what was
checked."

my visible setup, and wrote a deviation-aware handover."

6. Product definition

The product in one workflow
   1. Upload documents: SOP, SDS, equipment guide, optional lab rules.
   2. Compile protocol: AI extracts steps, hazards, PPE, checkpoints, controls, timers, required materials,
         expected observations, and stop conditions.
   3. Review graph: User sees a clean protocol graph and can approve or edit.
   4. Start run: System guides the user step by step.
   5. Ask hands-free questions: "What PPE applies here?" "What do I do if the reading is outside range?"
         "Where does the manual mention calibration?"
   6. Check setup by photo: User uploads a bench/setup photo. System checks only visible, source-grounded
         items.
   7. Log deviations: User says, "Sample 3 looked cloudy," or "Incubation started 4 minutes late."
   8. Escalate when needed: System says "Stop and consult supervisor" when a source-grounded stop condition
         appears.
   9. Generate handover: Timeline, completed steps, open items, deviations, photos, unresolved risks, and source
         citations.

What makes it "OS-like"

It has:

   · Compiler: Turns docs into structured executable state.
   · Runtime: Knows current step, allowed next steps, dependencies, timers, and checkpoints.
   · Verifier: Checks source-grounded conditions using text and photos.
   · Memory: Remembers the run history, user notes, deviations, and unresolved questions.
   · Handover layer: Produces an audit-style summary for the next human.

   · Escalation contacts or escalation categories.
   · Expected observations.
   · Data fields to capture.
   · Deviation fields.

A key product principle: every extracted item must point back to source evidence. No source, no claim.

Pillar 2: Hazard verifier

The hazard verifier cross-references procedure steps with SDS and safety docs. OSHA requires SDSs to include
structured information such as identification, hazard identification, first aid, fire-fighting, accidental release,
handling/storage, exposure controls, PPE, stability/reactivity, and toxicological information; this maps cleanly to the
verifier's extraction schema. (OSHA)

The hazard verifier should identify:

   · Step-specific hazards.
   · Required PPE.
   · Ventilation or containment requirements.
   · Incompatible materials.
   · Spill/accidental release actions.
   · First-aid notes.
   · Fire/ignition warnings.
   · Storage/handling constraints.
   · Disposal references.
   · Unknown or missing SDS coverage.

The verifier should never say, "This is safe." It should say:

      "Based on the uploaded SOP and SDS, this step requires X and Y. I can verify A from your photo, but I
      cannot verify B. Stop if condition C is present."

The execution coach is the live interface.

It should support:

   · "Start run."
   · "Next step."
   · "Repeat."
   · "What does this mean?"
   · "What PPE do I need for this step?"
   · "Show source."
   · "Log note."
   · "Start timer."
   · "Pause run."
   · "Mark deviation."

· "Ask supervisor."

The coach should be terse by default. In a lab, long answers are bad UX.

Preferred answer style:

"You're on Step 4: verify the hood and labels. Required before proceeding: goggles, gloves, lab coat,
active ventilation, labeled container. I can walk you through the checklist."

Pillar 4: Visual verifier

The visual verifier checks visible setup conditions against the protocol graph.

It should only claim what is visible:

· "I can see labeled tubes."
· "I can see a secondary containment tray."
· "I can see the balance display is on."
· "I cannot confirm the fume hood airflow from this image."
· "The reagent label is not readable; upload a closer image."

The National Academies notes that photographs can be effective inspection evidence and that inspection reports may
include photos of issues that require attention. That supports the product's "visual evidence + report" framing.
(National Academies)

The visual verifier should not infer hidden safety conditions:

· It should not infer chemical identity from bottle shape.
· It should not infer sterility.
· It should not infer ventilation performance unless a readable indicator is visible.
· It should not infer that PPE is compliant if the image does not show it.
· It should not identify people; it should evaluate setup elements.

11. Non-functional requirements

Safety

The system must behave as a decision-support layer, not an authority. It should surface source-grounded
requirements, uncertainty, and escalation triggers.

Safety UX rules:

   · Never say "safe to proceed" as an absolute.
   · Say "requirements from the uploaded SOP/SDS appear satisfied / not satisfied / not verifiable."
   · Always distinguish visible evidence from inferred evidence.
   · Require human confirmation at critical steps.
   · Escalate on missing documents, conflicting instructions, unreadable labels, unknown hazards, or user-

         proposed substitutions.
   · Refuse to provide new experimental designs or unapproved modifications.

Traceability

Every important claim should be traceable:

   · Which document?
   · Which page/section?
   · Which extracted field?
   · Which run event?
   · Which user confirmation?
   · Which photo?

Reliability

The runtime should not depend on the model remembering state in free-form chat. Store state in a deterministic
database/state machine.

Accessibility

The interface should support:

   · Voice commands.
   · Large text mode.
   · High contrast mode.
   · Minimal steps per screen.
   · Keyboard navigation.
   · Screen reader labels.
   · Text alternatives for images.
   · Adjustable response verbosity.
   · "Read this step aloud."
   · "Repeat last instruction."
   · "Summarize only critical actions."

WCAG 2.2 covers recommendations for making web content more accessible across visual, auditory, physical,
speech, cognitive, language, learning, and neurological disabilities; it is a useful baseline for the web UI. (W3C)

Privacy and security

Lab procedures may contain proprietary methods. MVP security should include:

   · No public sharing of uploaded docs.
   · Clear data retention policy.
   · Delete run/docs button.
   · No training on user-uploaded docs.
   · Basic auth for demo.
   · Separate user sessions.
   · Redaction option for names and sample IDs.

Latency

Target UX:

   · Step Q&A: under 5 seconds.
   · Visual check: under 15 seconds.
   · Protocol compile: under 60 seconds for demo docs.
   · Report generation: under 30 seconds.

Best demo mismatch

Use a mismatch that is:

   · Easy for the model to see.
   · Easy for judges to understand.
   · Safety-relevant but not alarmist.
   · Non-sensitive.

Good demo mismatches:

   · Unlabeled tube.
   · Missing secondary containment tray.
   · Missing waste container label.
   · Reagent bottle label unreadable.
   · Required tube count mismatch.
   · Balance not level or display not visible.
   · Timer not started.
   · Manual says cap should be closed; photo shows cap open.
   · Protocol says "use amber container"; photo shows clear container.

Avoid demo mismatches that require expertise or hidden state:

   · "This chemical is incompatible."
   · "This hood airflow is adequate."
   · "This sample is sterile."
   · "This person is wearing correct PPE."
   · "This material is safe."

Escalation language

Use direct, non-dramatic language:

      "Pause here. The uploaded SOP requires a labeled container before this step, and the photo does not show
      a readable label. I cannot verify this checkpoint. Please correct it or consult your supervisor before
      proceeding."

      "Stop the run and follow your lab's emergency procedure. I can show the uploaded SDS emergency
      section, but I cannot replace your institution's emergency response process."

Important safety boundary

OpenBench OS should not teach users how to perform hazardous experiments from scratch. It should interpret and
operationalize the documents the lab already approved.

The system's allowed behavior:

      "Here is what your SOP/SDS/manual says for this step."

The system's disallowed behavior:

      "Here is a new method you can use instead."

26. Judge-facing narrative

The pitch should emphasize four things:

1. Real pain

Labs are document-heavy, safety-sensitive, and physically constrained. The user is not sitting comfortably at a desk;
they are wearing gloves, handling equipment, switching contexts, and trying not to miss details.

2. AI is essential, not decorative

A normal app cannot easily read SOPs, SDSs, and manuals, compile them into a graph, answer context-aware
questions, inspect a photo, and write a handover. The model is central.

3. Multimodal advantage

The demo proves text + vision + memory + state. Opus 4.7's stronger image and long-running workflow capabilities
are directly aligned with the product concept. (Anthropic)

4. Human value

This helps safety, reproducibility, onboarding, accessibility, and handover. It is not a toy productivity app.

   1. Source first. Every answer should be traceable.
   2. Uncertainty is a feature. "Cannot verify" is better than guessing.
   3. Human remains responsible. The system supports, never authorizes.
   4. One step at a time. Lab UX must reduce cognitive load.

   8. Compile, don't chat. The graph is the product.

32. The "Keep Thinking" angle

The strongest version of this project is not "AI answers lab questions." It is:

      AI keeps thinking across the whole procedure: before the run, during execution, at visual
      checkpoints, and after the handover.

The system thinks in four phases:

   1. Before: Compile documents into a structured graph.
   2. During: Maintain run state and answer step-specific questions.
   3. At checkpoints: Verify visible setup conditions and surface uncertainty.
   4. After: Produce a handover report that remembers what happened.

That is a much stronger "Keep Thinking" interpretation than a chatbot. It demonstrates long-horizon context,
structured reasoning, tool use, multimodal verification, memory, and responsible uncertainty.
